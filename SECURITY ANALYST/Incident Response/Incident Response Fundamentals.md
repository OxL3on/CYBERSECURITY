# Incident Response



### Introduction to Incident Response

**Main Topic:**  
**Incident Response** is the process of planning, managing, and minimizing the impact of cyber security incidents, from prevention to recovery.


### **Key Points:**

1. **What is a Cyber Security Incident?**  
   - Any cyber attack or breach that causes harm (e.g., data loss, financial damage).  
   - Examples: Hacking, ransomware, data theft.  

2. **Why Incident Response is Important:**  
   - Just like securing your home with guards and cameras, organizations need proactive measures to prevent attacks.  
   - But if an attack happens, they also need a plan to respond effectively and reduce damage.  

3. **What Incident Response Covers:**  
   - **Prevention:** Deploying security measures (e.g., firewalls, antivirus).  
   - **Detection:** Identifying when an attack occurs.  
   - **Response:** Fighting the attack and minimizing its impact.  
   - **Recovery:** Restoring systems and learning from the incident.  

Imagine living in a heavily insecure street with many expensive things in your home. You must be thinking of having a security guard and a few CCTV cameras in your home. Hiding the expensive material inside a hidden underground room is a good idea if any intruder successfully enters the house. These are the things that you plan for the safety of your home, even before any attack occurs.

Besides these proactive measures, did you ever consider how things would work if someone successfully bypassed your external security mechanisms and gained access to your home? You must also take several other measures after your home is attacked.
**-----**  
Imagine your house is broken into:  
- **Prevention:** You installed CCTV and hid valuables in a secret room.  
- **Detection:** The alarm goes off when an intruder enters.  
- **Response:** You call the police and lock the intruder in one room.  
- **Recovery:** After the attack, you fix the broken door, review security, and improve it for the future.  

In the digital world, **Incident Response** is the same process but for cyber attacks. It’s like having a detailed plan for what to do before, during, and after a cyber attack.


**Remembering Tip:**  
Think of Incident Response as a **fire drill**:  
- **Before:** Set up alarms and fire extinguishers (prevention).  
- **During:** Put out the fire and evacuate safely (response).  
- **After:** Repair the damage and improve safety measures (recovery).

### What are Incidents?

**Main Topic:**  
**Incidents** are harmful activities identified by security solutions after analyzing alerts, categorized by severity to prioritize response.


### **Key Points:**

1. **Events and Alerts:**  
   - **Events:** Logs generated by processes on devices (e.g., file downloads, system updates).  
   - **Alerts:** Security solutions flag suspicious events for review.  

2. **Types of Alerts:**  
   - **False Positives:** Alerts that seem dangerous but are harmless (e.g., a backup process flagged as data theft).  
   - **True Positives:** Alerts that point to real threats (e.g., phishing emails).  

3. **What is an Incident?**  
   - A **True Positive** alert that confirms harmful activity becomes an **Incident**.  

4. **Incident Severity Levels:**  
   - **Critical:** Highest priority; major impact (e.g., ransomware attack).  
   - **High:** Serious but less urgent than critical.  
   - **Medium:** Moderate risk; needs attention but not urgent.  
   - **Low:** Minor issues; lowest priority.  


**-----**  
Imagine you’re a doctor in a busy hospital:  
- **Events:** Every heartbeat, cough, or sneeze is logged (like device logs).  
- **Alerts:** A machine beeps when something unusual happens (e.g., high fever).  
  - **False Positive:** A patient’s high temperature turns out to be from exercising, not illness.  
  - **True Positive:** A patient has a serious infection and needs immediate care.  
- **Severity:** You prioritize patients based on how sick they are—critical cases first!  

In cyber security:  
- **Events = Patient symptoms.**  
- **Alerts = Test results.**  
- **Incidents = Confirmed illnesses.**  
- **Severity = How fast you treat the patient.**


**Remembering Tip:**  
Think of incidents like a hospital triage system:  
- **False Positives = False alarms (no harm).**  
- **True Positives = Real emergencies (harmful).**  
- **Severity = Decide who gets treated first!**
